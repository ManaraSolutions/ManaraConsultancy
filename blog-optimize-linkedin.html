Optimize your LinkedIn.

The profile changes that put you in front of the right recruiters and clients: headline, about section, keywords and activity.

Manara ConsultancyAdvisory · Beirut & the Gulf
6 MIN READ
Share

Recruiters, clients and investors check LinkedIn before they reply to you. Most profiles read like a job description nobody asked for. A few deliberate changes fix that.

01 The headline is a search field

Your headline is the single biggest factor in whether you appear in searches and whether people click. Use the words your audience actually searches, not your internal job title.

02 The about section is a pitch

  • Open with who you help and how, in one line
  • Show proof: results, scope, recognisable names where allowed
  • Write in the first person, in your real voice
  • End with a clear next step for the reader

03 Keywords, quietly everywhere

Weave the terms you want to be found for into your headline, about, experience and skills. LinkedIn is a search engine; treat your profile like a page you want ranked.

04 Then show up

An optimised profile with no activity goes stale. A modest, consistent posting rhythm keeps you visible, which is exactly what a planned content calendar makes sustainable.

05 The photo and banner do quiet work

Before anyone reads a word, they have judged your photo. A clear, current, professional headshot and a banner that states what you do are not vanity — they are the first two seconds of trust. An empty grey banner and a distant, poorly lit photo quietly tell visitors you are not really here. These are the easiest wins on the page, and the most overlooked.

06 Featured is your proof shelf

The Featured section sits high on your profile and most people leave it empty. Fill it with your best evidence — an article you wrote, a case study, a talk, a result you are proud of. It is the one place you can show rather than tell, and it turns a profile that claims credibility into one that demonstrates it.

Recommendations still matter

A written recommendation from a client or colleague is social proof no self-description can match. A handful of specific, recent recommendations — each naming what you actually did — does more for trust than another paragraph about yourself. Ask the people whose projects went well; most are glad to, especially if you offer to write one in return.
